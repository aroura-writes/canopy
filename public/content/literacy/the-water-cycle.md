# The Water Cycle

Water is the most extraordinary substance on Earth. The same molecules that fill your glass today may once have been part of a glacier, a cloud, a dinosaur, or an ancient ocean. The water cycle — also called the hydrological cycle — is the continuous journey of water through Earth's systems.

## The Journey Begins: Evaporation

The sun drives the water cycle. Solar energy heats the surface of oceans, lakes, and rivers, causing water molecules to escape into the atmosphere as vapor. This process — evaporation — lifts approximately 500,000 cubic kilometers of water from the oceans each year.

Plants contribute too, through a process called **transpiration**. A single large oak tree can transpire over 40,000 gallons of water per year, releasing it through tiny pores in its leaves called stomata.

## Rising and Cooling: Condensation

As water vapor rises into cooler altitudes, it condenses around tiny particles of dust, pollen, or sea salt. These microscopic water droplets gather by the billions to form clouds. A single cumulus cloud can contain hundreds of tons of water.

> The shapes and heights of clouds tell us about the atmosphere's mood — wispy cirrus clouds indicate fair weather, while towering cumulonimbus clouds signal storms.

## Returning to Earth: Precipitation

When cloud droplets merge and grow heavy enough, they fall as precipitation — rain, snow, sleet, or hail. Earth receives about 505,000 cubic kilometers of precipitation annually. Most falls over the oceans, but the precipitation that reaches land is what sustains terrestrial life.

## The Ground Journey: Collection and Infiltration

Once precipitation reaches the ground, it takes several paths:

- **Surface runoff** — water flows over the land into streams, rivers, and eventually back to the ocean
- **Infiltration** — water seeps into the soil, replenishing groundwater aquifers
- **Glacial storage** — in cold regions, snow accumulates into glaciers and ice sheets, locking water away for centuries or millennia

Groundwater is a hidden treasure. Aquifers beneath the Earth's surface hold 30% of the planet's freshwater — far more than all the world's rivers and lakes combined.

## The Numbers

- 97.5% of Earth's water is saltwater in the oceans
- 2.5% is freshwater, but most of that is locked in ice caps and glaciers
- Only 0.3% of all water on Earth is accessible freshwater in rivers, lakes, and shallow aquifers
- The total amount of water on Earth has remained virtually constant for billions of years

## Water and Climate Change

Climate change is disrupting the water cycle in profound ways. Warmer air holds more moisture, leading to more intense rainfall and flooding in some regions, while accelerating evaporation and drought in others. Glaciers — the world's natural water towers — are shrinking, threatening water supplies for billions of people.

## Protecting Our Water

Every drop matters. Here's how to be a better water steward:

- Fix household leaks — a dripping faucet wastes over 3,000 gallons per year
- Water gardens in the early morning to reduce evaporation
- Choose drought-resistant native plants
- Protect local waterways from pollution and erosion
