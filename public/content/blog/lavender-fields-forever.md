# Lavender Fields Forever

Few plants capture the imagination quite like lavender. With its distinctive purple spikes and calming fragrance, lavender has been cherished for over 2,500 years — used in everything from ancient Egyptian mummification rituals to modern aromatherapy.

## Ancient Roots

The word "lavender" derives from the Latin *lavare*, meaning "to wash." The Romans were particularly fond of lavender, adding it to their bathwater for its cleansing scent and believed healing properties. Roman soldiers carried lavender with them on campaigns to dress wounds and ward off disease.

## The Fields of Provence

No image is more synonymous with lavender than the endless purple rows of Provence, France. Commercial lavender cultivation in this region began in the early 19th century, initially for the perfume industry centered in nearby Grasse.

> The peak bloom season falls between mid-June and mid-August, when the Valensole plateau transforms into an ocean of violet under the Mediterranean sun.

The two main species cultivated are *Lavandula angustifolia* (true lavender, grown at higher altitudes) and *lavandin* — a more productive hybrid grown in the lowlands. Together, France produces roughly 50 tons of lavender essential oil per year.

## Medicinal History

Throughout the Middle Ages, lavender was considered a cure-all. Hildegard von Bingen, the 12th-century mystic and herbalist, prescribed lavender water for migraines. During the Great Plague of London in 1665, glove makers who scented their wares with lavender oil were said to be mysteriously immune to the disease.

Modern science has validated some of these ancient claims. Studies show that lavender essential oil can reduce anxiety, improve sleep quality, and even act as a mild analgesic.

## Culinary Uses

Lavender is not just for sachets and soaps. In French cuisine, it's a key ingredient in *herbes de Provence*. Lavender honey, produced by bees that feed primarily on lavender blossoms, is prized for its delicate floral flavor. Lavender-infused ice cream, shortbread, and lemonade have become beloved treats worldwide.

## Growing Your Own

Lavender thrives in well-drained, alkaline soil with full sun. It's remarkably drought-tolerant once established, making it an ideal choice for water-conscious gardens. The key to healthy lavender is avoiding overwatering — this Mediterranean native prefers dry feet.
